# Technical Design Standards: Color

Color is a strategic tool for functional requirements and brand personality. When project essence is defined, color identity can be derived systematically.

## 1. Core Attributes

- **Hue:** Position on the spectrum (e.g. blue, red, yellow)—differentiates concepts and highlights.
- **Saturation:** Intensity/purity vs gray. Full saturation = maximum chromatic impact.
- **Value:** Lightness/darkness via **tints** (add white) and **shades** (add black). Drives mood and atmosphere.

**Tonal roles:** Primary (boldness, impact) → pure primaries. Secondary (vibrancy, uniqueness) → equal mix of two primaries. Tertiary (musicality, narrative) → primary + adjacent secondary.

## 2. Irradiation and Perceptual Geometry

Luminosity alters perceived size and depth. Account for it or risk unbalanced hierarchy.

- **Light on dark:** Lighter colors appear larger and "come forward"—high-contrast focal points.
- **Dark on light:** On white/light canvas, darker colors read larger in context.
- **Luminosity-based depth:** High-luminosity (e.g. yellow) stands out on dark cool (e.g. blue) but can recede against similar values (e.g. light greens).

## 3. Tonal Hierarchy: "Shout and Whisper"

- **High-value contrast:** Bold, energetic, highly legible—use for CTAs and primary branding.
- **Low-value contrast:** "Glow" or "vibration"—use sparingly; can make type a "lively texture" rather than strict readability.
- **Shouting:** High-contrast pairings for full attention.
- **Whispering:** Monochromatic or analogous for fluidity and sophistication.

## 4. Relational Color Combinations

| Type | So what? | Technique | Use |
|------|----------|-----------|-----|
| **Complementary** | Max dynamism, contrast | Opposites on wheel (e.g. red/green) | Bold, balanced presence |
| **Analogous** | Cohesion, harmony | Three adjacent hues | Soothing, unified layouts |
| **Triadic** | Diversity + consistency | Three equidistant hues | Varieties within one family |
| **Monochromatic** | Depth, contemporary | One hue + tints/shades | Restricted ink/budget |

## 5. Palette Development Protocol

1. **Intuitive selection:** Start from "brand essence" and required feeling.
2. **Isolation testing:** Place colors in equal-sized squares; judge equilibrium without type or composition.
3. **Iteration:** If one color is too loud or soft, shift the palette one step (saturation/value); observe effect.
4. **Categorization:** Core palette (primary drivers) vs supporting palette (accents). Balance proportion and tension vs softness.

## 6. Environmental and Contextual Variability

- **Light:** Same hue can shift (e.g. yellow → green) under different lighting; design for context (e.g. dark underpass vs bright gallery).
- **Size, shape, neighbors:** A color can recede or advance depending on adjacency.
- **Cultural connotations and color memory:** Align with target audience associations and lived experience.

Use color as both scientifically understood and intuitively felt to drive specific thought and action.
