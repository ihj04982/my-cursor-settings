# Internal Framework: Dynamic Layouts

Composition is the "magical layout principle" that bridges idea and message. A successful layout must: attract attention, deliver a clear message, and communicate story and tone.

## Strategic Goals

- **Clarity of idea:** Communicate one specific idea as clearly as possible.
- **Intentionality:** Every element has a reason; otherwise it is noise.
- **Active engagement:** Invite the audience to participate, not just observe.
- **Hierarchy management:** Guide the eye in intended order (first, second, third).

## 1. Structural Foundations: Lines and Shapes

| Element | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Thicker lines (rules) | Importance, attention, texture, boundary. |
| Rounded shapes | Softness, calm, stability. |
| Sharp/angular shapes | Assertiveness, agility, energy. |
| Implied shapes (e.g. text blocks) | Authority, stability. |
| Repeated shapes | Recognizable language, familiarity. |

**Checklist:** Does each shape carry meaning or just fill space? Is Western top-down reading order respected? Does shape "feeling" match project tone? Do lines separate information or add noise?

## 2. Spatial Intelligence: Negative Space and Scale

- **Less is more:** Filling every inch creates a "screaming" effect; negative space lets elements breathe and amplifies content.
- **Refining clutter:** (1) Audit for essentiality—remove what doesn't add to the message. (2) Consolidate to one or two focal points. (3) Shrink non-essential text to reclaim silence.
- **Extreme scale:** Avoid safe middle ground. Push scale boldly (e.g. +200% or "almost doesn't fit"); or make something so small it's nearly missed. Contrast creates hierarchy.

## 3. Tension and Contrast

- **Off-center:** Center is static; edge placement activates the page.
- **Proximity/gravity:** Move elements until they almost touch or overlap so they "react" to each other.
- **Suggested motion:** Rotation, edge-alignment, oversized type "sliding off" the page.
- **Avoid equal space trap:** Equal margins = bland; intentional overpowering of margins can create productive discomfort.

**Contrast pairings:** Big vs small; dark vs light (e.g. white on black reads brighter/larger); vibrant vs dull (push saturation); complex vs simple.

## 4. Repetition as Design Language

- **Functional repetition:** Consistent headlines, page numbers, column widths—prevents frustration.
- **Aesthetic repetition:** Shared shapes/icons across a series for harmony.
- **Conceptual repetition:** e.g. repeated phrase as "Voice of the Crowd"—repetition with meaning, not filler.

## 5. Design Review Checklist

- **Communication:** Does the layout aggressively support the primary idea? Is every element intentional?
- **Hierarchy:** Is reading order clear? Do lines/shapes guide the eye?
- **Space & scale:** Enough "silence"? Scale pushed to the "uncomfortable" threshold?
- **Energy:** Intentional tension (off-center, proximity, motion)? Avoiding safe center?
- **Consistency:** Does repetition serve function or concept, or create mediocrity?

Practice iteration; compare to past work; push saturation, scale, and tension. Never settle for safe.
